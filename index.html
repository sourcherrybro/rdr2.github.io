America, 1899. The end of the Wild West era has begun. After a robbery goes badly wrong in the western town of Blackwater, Arthur Morgan and the Van der Linde gang are forced to flee. With federal agents and the best bounty hunters in the nation massing on their heels, the gang must rob, steal and fight their way across the rugged heartland of America in order to survive. As deepening internal divisions threaten to tear the gang apart, Arthur must make a choice between his own ideals and loyalty to the gang who raised him.

Red Dead Redemption 2 is a 2018 action-adventure game developed and published by Rockstar Games. Set in 1899, it follows Arthur Morgan, an outlaw in the Van der Linde gang, as he navigates the decline of the Wild West. The game features an open world, moral choices, and a mix of combat, exploration, and story-driven missions.

The world of Red Dead Redemption 2 covers five fictional U.S. states: Ambarino (mountain wilderness), New Hanover (valleys and towns like Valentine), Lemoyne (bayous and plantations, including Saint Denis), West Elizabeth (plains, forests, and Blackwater), and New Austin (arid desert with Armadillo and Tumbleweed). Each region features unique landscapes and settlements, with some areas redesigned to reflect the earlier time period.

Arthur Morgan, an enforcer in the Van der Linde gang led by Dutch van der Linde, is the main character. The gang includes figures like Hosea Matthews, John Marston, Abigail Roberts, Uncle, Bill Williamson, Sean MacGuire, Javier Escuella, Micah Bell, Charles Smith, and Sadie Adler. Their criminal activities put them at odds with oil tycoon Leviticus Cornwall, the Pinkerton Detective Agency, rival gangs, and various powerful figures. Arthur also aids Native American leaders Rains Fall and Eagle Flies as their land is threatened by the U.S. Army.

Gameplay

Red Dead Redemption 2 is a Western-themed action-adventure game set in an open-world environment inspired by the United States in 1899. Played from a first- or third-person perspective, it includes both single-player and online multiplayer modes (the latter known as Red Dead Online).

For most of the game, players control Arthur Morgan, an outlaw and member of the Van der Linde gang, completing story-driven missions with set objectives. In the epilogue, control shifts to John Marston, the protagonist of the original Red Dead Redemption. In Red Dead Redemption 2, players explore a vast world with diverse landscapes, ranging from towns to wilderness. Horses are the primary means of transport, with players able to steal or tame them. They can bond with horses, improving performance. Stagecoaches and trains can also be hijacked for loot.

The world features dynamic events like crimes, animal attacks, and random encounters. Players can engage in various side activities, including hunting, gambling, and dueling. Hunting provides food, income, and materials, with the choice of weapon affecting the quality of the kill.

Player choices impact the story, with the Honor system influencing NPC reactions and rewards. High Honor grants discounts and special outfits, while low Honor offers more loot. The Dead Eye system allows players to slow time and target enemies. The character’s health, stamina, and appearance are influenced by food, sleep, and clothing. Weapons require maintenance and use-specific upgrades. Committing crimes leads to bounties, lawmen, and bounty hunters chasing the player, with the option to pay off the bounty or spend time in jail.

Development

Red Dead Redemption 2's development began shortly after the original game's release in 2010. By mid-2011, a rough outline was in place, and by late 2012, the script was complete. Rockstar Games combined its studios into one large team of around 2,000 people to develop the game. The budget was estimated at $370–540 million, making it one of the most expensive games ever created.

While the first game focused on protecting family, Red Dead Redemption 2 explores the breakdown of the Van der Linde gang. Inspired by historical fiction, the team wanted to create an accurate reflection of the time, contrasting rich and poor, civilization and wilderness. Rockstar wanted to explore why the gang fell apart, as hinted in the first game. The writers focused on each character's backstory and their reasons for staying with the gang. The player controls one character instead of multiple protagonists, allowing for a more personal connection to the story.

The game was designed for the PlayStation 4 and Xbox One. Rockstar aimed to make the player feel immersed in a living world, with interactive characters and realistic environments. The music, composed by Woody Jackson, features narrative, interactive, and environmental scores, reacting to player actions.

The team faced intense "crunch time" during development, with senior writers working 100-hour weeks, though Rockstar clarified it was only for a few weeks and not mandatory for all staff. By 2020, many employees felt that conditions had improved.

Red Dead Redemption 2 was teased in 2016 and delayed twice before its release on October 26, 2018. A companion app was released alongside the game, providing additional features. The Windows version, released in November 2019, included technical improvements.

Synopsis

Settings and characters

Red Dead Redemption 2 takes place across five fictional U.S. states. Ambarino is a mountain wilderness with the Wapiti Native American reservation, while New Hanover features towns like Valentine and Annesburg. Lemoyne is a bayou region, home to Rhodes and Saint Denis (inspired by New Orleans). West Elizabeth includes the port town of Blackwater and Strawberry, a mountain resort. New Austin is a desert region, with towns like Armadillo and Tumbleweed, reflecting the earlier time period. The story follows Arthur Morgan, a loyal but conflicted member of the Van der Linde gang, led by the charismatic but increasingly unstable Dutch van der Linde. Other gang members include Hosea Matthews, Dutch's co-leader, John Marston (from the first game), Abigail Roberts, Sadie Adler, Bill Williamson, and Micah Bell, among others. The gang faces challenges from oil tycoon Leviticus Cornwall, the Pinkerton Detective Agency, and various enemies, including Mafia boss Angelo Bronte and corrupt Governor Fussar. As tensions rise, Arthur helps Native Americans like Rains Fall and Eagle Flies, while dealing with betrayal and moral dilemmas within the gang.

Plot

In May 1899, following a failed ferry heist in the bustling port town of Blackwater, Dutch Van der Linde and his gang are forced to flee. The heist goes awry, leaving one member, Jenny, dead, and Davey seriously wounded (eventually dying from his injuries). Davey's brother, Mac, gets separated, and Sean MacGuire is captured by bounty hunters working for Ike Skelding. The gang decides to escape through the treacherous snowy mountains to avoid the law, leaving their considerable fortune behind in Blackwater. The gang's members include Arthur Morgan, John Marston, Hosea Matthews, Bill Williamson, Javier Escuella, Lenny Summers, Charles Smith, Uncle, Abigail Roberts, Susan Grimshaw, Molly O'Shea, Tilly Jackson, Simon Pearson, Leopold Strauss, Karen Jones, Mary-Beth Gaskill, Reverend Swanson, and Micah Bell. They set up camp in the abandoned mining town of Colter, using it as a temporary refuge while they regroup. During their time in Colter, Arthur, Micah, and Dutch discover a nearby homestead hosting a party. However, they soon realize the party is being thrown by the O'Driscolls, a rival gang. After a violent confrontation, the trio manages to eliminate the O'Driscolls and rescue Sadie Adler, a widow whose husband was murdered by the gang. Grateful for their help, Sadie joins Dutch’s gang, and the group begins to bond with her as they prepare for their next move.

To fund their escape south, Dutch orders the gang to rob a train owned by the wealthy oil magnate, Leviticus Cornwall, despite Hosea’s objections. The heist goes as planned, but Cornwall, furious and vengeful, hires the Pinkerton Detective Agency to track down the gang. Agents Andrew Milton and Edgar Ross are dispatched to apprehend them, escalating the danger for Dutch and his crew. Seeing the looming threat of civilization encroaching on their outlaw lifestyle, Dutch realizes that the era of free-roaming outlaws is coming to an end. He pushes the gang to gather enough money for one final escape and promises them that they will soon be able to retire and live without fear of the law. The gang relocates to the state of New Hanover, setting up camp on the plateau of Horseshoe Overlook, where they continue to plan and execute various jobs and heists in their quest for the money needed to secure their freedom. Dutch repeatedly promises the gang that one last, big heist will give them everything they need, and they will finally be able to escape the law. During this time, they also plan to rescue Sean MacGuire from the bounty hunters, thanks to the unexpected return of Josiah Trelawny, a gang member who had been absent for months. His return signals hope for the gang, as they see it as another opportunity to regain what they’ve lost.

After a deadly shootout with Leviticus Cornwall’s men in Valentine and a tense confrontation between Arthur and Pinkerton agents Milton and Ross outside the gang’s camp, Dutch realizes the situation has become increasingly perilous. The gang is forced to move again, this time relocating to the state of Lemoyne, setting up camp at Clemens Point, a more secluded area in the bayou. In Lemoyne, the gang encounters the Grays and the Braithwaites, two powerful rival families who are rumored to be hoarding valuable Civil War gold. Dutch sees an opportunity to pit the two families against each other, so the gang works for both, manipulating each side to fuel their rivalry. However, the plan backfires when both families discover they've been played, and things take a deadly turn. The Grays, enraged by the betrayal, ambush the gang in Rhodes, resulting in the death of Sean MacGuire. To make matters worse, the Braithwaites retaliate by kidnapping John Marston's young son, Jack. In a fit of fury and desperation, the gang decides to exact brutal revenge on both families. They launch a violent assault, slaughtering the remaining members of the Grays and Braithwaites in retaliation. Through the chaos, the gang learns that Jack has been sold to the notorious crime lord Angelo Bronte, who resides in the city of Saint Denis. Realizing the urgency of the situation, Dutch and the gang prepare to head to the city to rescue Jack, while tensions within the group continue to rise as the weight of their increasingly violent actions takes its toll.

The shootout in Rhodes and the assault on Braithwaite Manor led Milton and Ross to discover the gang’s camp. Once the gang is safely relocated to Shady Belle, Dutch, John, and Arthur head to Bronte's mansion in Saint Denis to confront him and retrieve Jack. Bronte returns Jack and embraces the gang, but then leads them into a trap. In retaliation, Dutch, against Hosea's advice and pleas, kidnaps Bronte with the gang and feeds him to an alligator, murdering him. At this point, Dutch has grown convinced that there is no future for the gang in the United States and leads them into a Saint Denis bank robbery to gather enough money to move overseas. Attracted in the city following Bronte’s kidnapping, the Pinkertons intervene, and Milton executes Hosea in front of the gang. A shootout ensues, in which Lenny is killed and John is arrested. Arthur, Dutch, Bill, Javier, and Micah escape on a boat to Cuba. A storm sinks the ship, and the gang washes ashore on Guarma, where they get involved in a revolution against oppressive sugar plantation owners. They succeed in the revolt and return to the U.S., reuniting with the gang at Lakay.

As the story progresses, Dutch’s paranoia and manipulation reach a boiling point. His growing distrust of Arthur, who has long been one of his closest allies, is exacerbated by Micah Bell, who consistently fuels Dutch’s insecurities and whispers doubts about Arthur’s loyalty. Micah, who has quietly risen in prominence since Hosea’s death, positions himself as the gang’s moral compass to Dutch, undermining Arthur at every turn. Dutch’s vulnerability, combined with Micah’s persuasive words, leads him to question not only Arthur’s commitment to the gang but also his role as a leader. Dutch’s obsession with one final heist, and his increasingly erratic decision-making, become more dangerous. He believes that a massive score will resolve all their problems—he convinces himself that this one last big job will allow the gang to escape the law for good. His blind pursuit of that goal blinds him to the reality of their situation. The gang is on the verge of collapse, and Dutch’s refusal to acknowledge the severity of their circumstances only accelerates their demise. Meanwhile, Arthur becomes increasingly disillusioned with Dutch. His decision to rescue John Marston—despite Dutch’s insistence that they must wait—is a pivotal moment in the story. Arthur, who once viewed Dutch as a father figure and the leader of their family, can no longer ignore the man’s dangerous descent into madness. Arthur knows that rescuing John is not just about saving his friend; it's a way of preserving whatever is left of their fractured sense of family. Sadie Adler, who shares Arthur’s distrust of Dutch, joins the rescue effort, and the two embark on a mission to free John, defying Dutch’s authority. While Arthur and Sadie are away, Dutch’s plan to manipulate a Native American tribe into conflict with the United States Army further exemplifies his descent into madness. He views this conflict as a way to distract the authorities and protect the gang, but Arthur—who is no longer blind to Dutch’s destructive tendencies—vehemently opposes the plan. The conflict highlights Dutch’s moral decline; he is willing to sacrifice innocent lives and provoke violence just to ensure the gang’s survival.

Arthur becomes increasingly concerned that Dutch has lost his moral compass, resorting to violence, paranoia, and revenge. Arthur later discovers he has contracted tuberculosis from a debt collection job with a diseased farmer, Thomas Downes, and realizes he has little time to live. Faced with his mortality, Arthur reflects on his past actions and seeks ways to protect the gang after his death.

Several gang members leave, disillusioned, while Dutch and Micah plan one last heist. Arthur convinces John to leave and start a new life after the heist. Dutch kills Cornwall, blaming him for the Pinkertons' pursuit. Arthur’s faith in Dutch is shattered when Dutch abandons John during the heist and refuses to rescue Abigail. Arthur disobeys Dutch, rescues Abigail with Sadie's help, and learns from Milton that Micah had been a Pinkerton informant since Guarma before Milton is killed by Abigail.

Arthur confronts Micah at camp, accusing him of being a Pinkerton informant, but Dutch denies it. The gang fractures, with John and Susan siding with Arthur. Micah kills Susan during the chaos. Dutch demands everyone choose sides, with Bill, Javier, Micah, Joe, and Cleet joining him against Arthur and John. The Pinkertons, led by Edgar Ross, attack, forcing the gang to flee. Arthur either confronts the Pinkertons to give John time to escape or returns to camp for Dutch's money. After either choice, Micah ambushes Arthur, and Dutch intervenes. Dutch abandons both men, and Micah leaves in anger. Arthur either dies honorably from tuberculosis or dishonorably at Micah’s hands, depending on the player's actions.

After reaching Micah's camp, Charles and Sadie are wounded, forcing John to continue the fight alone. He takes down several of Micah's men, including Joe, before confronting Micah, with Sadie closing in on him. Unexpectedly, Dutch returns, having rejoined Micah in an attempt to recover the Blackwater money. In a tense standoff, Micah takes Sadie hostage. Dutch accuses John of betraying the gang, but in a surprising move, Dutch shoots Micah for his own treachery. As Micah attempts to kill both John and Sadie, John brings an end to the violence once and for all. Afterward, John tries to express his gratitude to Dutch, but Dutch remains distant, ignoring him entirely as he walks away. With Micah dead, John claims the Blackwater money and uses it to settle his debt. Finally, John marries Abigail, and they look forward to building a peaceful life on their ranch, with Uncle and Jack by their side, while Charles and Sadie part ways to pursue their own futures.

The fates of the surviving gang members are revealed through various means such as newspaper clippings, letters, NPC dialogue, random encounters, and mid-credit scenes. Pearson settles down as the owner of a general store in Rhodes, Swanson becomes a priest in New York, and Tilly marries a lawyer, settling in Saint Denis. Mary-Beth achieves success as a romance novelist, while Karen's life tragically ends due to alcoholism. Strauss is arrested and dies in captivity, staying true to the gang's code by revealing none of their secrets. Meanwhile, Agent Ross, now working for the Bureau of Investigation, stumbles upon Micah’s body and begins tracking the trail, which leads him to John's ranch. He and his partner, Archer Fordham, start spying on John, setting the stage for the events of Red Dead Redemption four years later.

Upon completing the game 100%, a final scene plays where John Marston visits Arthur Morgan's grave. Standing before it, John solemnly remarks, "I guess we're just about done, my friend."

Reception

Critical response

Red Dead Redemption 2 received universal acclaim, ranking among the highest-rated games on Metacritic, alongside Grand Theft Auto V. Critics praised its story, characters, open world, graphics, and attention to detail. Game Informer's Matt Bertz called it Rockstar's most cohesive adventure, while The Guardian's Keza MacDonald referred to it as a "landmark game." IGN's Luke Reilly deemed it "one of the greatest games of the modern age." The game was also celebrated for its artistic value, with The New York Times’ Peter Suderman comparing its narrative depth to iconic works like The Godfather and The Sopranos.

Critics praised Red Dead Redemption 2's narrative for its boldness and unpredictability. GamesRadar's David Meikleham called it "perhaps the boldest triple-A game ever made," while The Guardian's Keza MacDonald admired the story's twists and the integration of smaller narratives. EGM's Nick Plessas noted that the best stories are found in the game's margins, driven by the player. Game Informer's Matt Bertz praised the narrative's consistency, despite its vast scope, though he wished for quieter moments. However, GameSpot's Kallie Plagge found parts of the story predictable, though important for Arthur’s arc. Giant Bomb's Alex Navarro criticized the portrayal of Native American characters and side missions. Some reviewers also pointed out the game's slow start and lengthy epilogue.

EGM's Nick Plessas found Arthur's redemption arc more compelling than John’s in the first game, as his sins made him more sympathetic. However, Eurogamer's Martin Robinson felt Arthur was less engaging than Marston, leading to a somewhat confusing narrative. GameSpot's Kallie Plagge praised the new characters for enriching the story, and USgamer's Mike Williams noted they felt like real people due to their diverse personalities. IGN's Luke Reilly commended the cultural variety and avoided clichés, while Giant Bomb's Alex Navarro appreciated the nuanced portrayal of Arthur’s inner conflict. The Guardian's Keza MacDonald found the performances made the characters more believable. Polygon's Chris Plante praised the political commentary surrounding the Braithwaite and Gray families but criticized the portrayal of Native American characters as insensitive. Lastly, Polygon's Eirik Gumeny admired the unflinching depiction of tuberculosis, particularly the harsh and misguided reactions from others.

Critics widely praised Red Dead Redemption 2 for its open world, calling it one of the best in gaming. EGM's Nick Plessas stated it "pushes industry boundaries in both size and detail," while The Guardian's Keza MacDonald admired its lifelike representation of American landscapes. IGN's Luke Reilly found it more expansive, beautiful, and varied than the first game, with each environment feeling vibrant and alive. GameSpot's Kallie Plagge was captivated by the world’s variety, interactivity, and hidden surprises. GamesRadar's David Meikleham even called it "the best-looking video game of all time," thanks to its incredible lighting and weather systems, with USgamer's Mike Williams agreeing, noting it was one of the best-looking games on PS4 and Xbox One. IGN's Reilly praised the advanced lighting engine and facial animations, while Game Informer's Matt Bertz highlighted the attention to detail, particularly the dynamic weather, ambient sounds, and the game’s sophisticated ecology.
